A 15-year-old Malay male presented with dizziness and vomiting for 1 month associated with a mild headache. He sought medical attention at general practitioners a few times for persistent symptoms. Otherwise, he denied limb weakness, imbalance gait, seizure, blurred vision, and tinnitus. He is a well-performing student with no history of substance use or previous trauma. On examination, the patient achieved the maximum Glasgow coma scale (GCS) score with no focal neurological deficit.
This patient presented to a district hospital in October 2018 with a terrible headache. Urgent computed tomography (CT) of the brain showed cerebellar hemorrhage with perilesional edema . The bleeding was located at the cerebellar vermis and caused a mass effect on the fourth ventricle and bilateral cerebellar peduncle. In addition, there were multiple small enhancing lesions in the cerebellar region adjacent to the hemorrhage .
He was immediately referred to the neurosurgical unit of Universiti Sains Malaysia (USM) Hospital and admitted to this unit for further management. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the brain performed 4 days later showed features of acute hemorrhage . Numerous well-defined, rounded blooming artifacts of varying sizes were seen in the left cerebellum, adjacent to the hemorrhage, favoring cavernomas. A tubular tangle of blood vessels was seen giving rise to the “caput medusae” sign . A large vein was seen from this region draining into the straight sinus. The patient was started on dexamethasone 4 mg, four times a day (QID), observed in the ward, and discharged well afterwards. On follow-up, the symptoms were resolved. The patient was offered surgical intervention, but he refused. He was well under neurosurgical follow-up until he relapsed 10 months later.
