An eight-year-old girl was brought to our hospital by her parents because of progressive deformity of the right fifth finger. Her mother reported to have noted progressive volar curvature of the distal aspect of the right fifth finger over a 3 to 4 months period. There was no history of injury, laceration or trauma of any sort. The patient had no fevers or erythema about the finger. Physical examination revealed extensor lag of the DIP joint and volar curvature of the distal phalanx consistent with a mallet finger. Initial radiographs revealed abnormal volar and radial curvature of the distal phalanx of the right fifth finger with widening of the epiphyseal plate . Initial differential considerations included a Salter Harris fracture, osteomyelitis and a congenital deformity such as Kirner’s deformity. However, in the absence of trauma and clinical signs and symptoms of infection, possibilities of fracture and infection were considered to be less likely. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was performed to further evaluate the deformity and exclude the possibility of infection. MR images demonstrated the abnormal curvatures of the distal phalanx without evidence for underlying osteomyelitis. No abnormal soft tissue enhancement was seen. There was normal insertion of the flexor digitorum profundus (FDP) tendon , contrary to the findings on some case reports in the literature. The patient was then treated conservatively, with no significant change in the deformity on follow up evaluations. The patient is currently being considered for possible corrective osteotomy.
