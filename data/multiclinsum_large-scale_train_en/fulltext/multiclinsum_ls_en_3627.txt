A 72-year-old male was admitted because a "colon tumor was found by colonoscopy examination for 2 wk".
Two weeks before admission, the patient was examined in the outpatient clinic. He did not have abdominal pain, fullness, diarrhea, hematochezia, fever, cough, chest tightness, or other discomfort.
The patient had a negative previous medical history.
The patient denied any family history.
The clinical examination revealed tenderness in his abdomen, and no lump was palpable. His general condition was otherwise good.
Initial laboratory testing showed no abnormalities.
Computed tomography examination showed that all organs in the chest and abdomen were inverted and that the wall of the colonic splenic flexure was thickened. Colon cancer was considered, and the surrounding lymph nodes are shown in Figure . Colonoscopy was performed, and a neoplasm was found 50 cm from the anus. The neoplasm had surface ulceration and nodular changes involving the intestinal wall in approximately 2/3 of the circular folds. The pathological examination revealed adenocarcinoma of the colon. The patient was admitted to the hospital with "colon cancer".
