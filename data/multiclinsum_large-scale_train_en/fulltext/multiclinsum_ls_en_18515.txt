A 54-year-old man presented with a history of a left frontal cutaneous cell carcinoma. The patient had surgical removal of the tumor followed by radiation therapy in 2000. He had no metastasis at that time. He was then lost to follow-up for more than a decade. He presented with a painful and ulcerated extensive left frontal cutaneous lesion which occasionally bled. The progressive growth of the lesion was neglected by the patient for years. Because of a rapid progression in the past 3 months, he consulted. No other symptoms were reported. Physical examination showed a 12 × 15 × 14 cm left frontal cauliflower-like mass with a necrotic center and peripheral subcutaneous induration with erythema . There was no neurological impairment. CT scan showed destruction of the left frontal skull . MRI revealed extensive frontal dural induration with a cystic invasion of the left frontal lobe [ and ]. The patient underwent surgical removal of the tumor. The invaded skin, bone, and dura were excised with a safety margin of 1 cm . The tumor that invaded the cortex was then removed. Fascia lata muscle was used for dural reconstruction and cement for frontal bone reconstruction. Cutaneous reconstruction was performed using a scalp graft and free thigh flap transfer [ and ]. Pathological examination revealed invasive SCC with negative margins. Three months after surgery, the grafts had completely healed . Local radiation therapy was then performed. Clinical follow-up showed no evidence of recurrence 2 years after surgery.
