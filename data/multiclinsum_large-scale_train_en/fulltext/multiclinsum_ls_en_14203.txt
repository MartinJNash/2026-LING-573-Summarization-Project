A 10-year-old girl reported to the pediatric dental clinic with a chief complaint of a missing front tooth and gave a history of exfoliation of a milk tooth in the same region one year back. There was no history of trauma. Family history and medical history were noncontributory. The patient had good general health, and their parents did not report any history of systemic disease or immune-compromised status. Intraoral features are mixed dentition with class I molar relation. Partially erupted lateral incisor, almost occupying half of the space for 11 . Distal to it, the incisal tip of its supplemental twin was present. Radiographic examination depicted an unerupted and mesiobuccally rotated right maxillary permanent central incisor with an immature root . Among the two lateral incisors, the mesial one showed a slightly curved root with some evidence of root resorption. A routine hematological examination did not reveal any abnormal findings.
On detailed examination, the presence of a high-arched palate and hypertrophy of mid-palatine raphe was noted. It was hypothesized that this could be a case of an unusual habit of tongue-sucking. On detailed inquiry, the parent reported that the child performs tongue-sucking by holding the tongue as if sucking on a lozenge, especially during sleeping hours. The parent noticed the habit because of the sucking sound during sleep and less frequently during waking hours. This habit was dated by the parent as being definitely practiced in the last 2 years at a minimum and probably even earlier ( and ).
