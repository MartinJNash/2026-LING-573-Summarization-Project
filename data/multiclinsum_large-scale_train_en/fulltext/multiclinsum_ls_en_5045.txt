A 74-year-old man developed aggravation of dysphagia approximately 13.5 mo after stent insertion following resection of esophageal cancer 6 mo previously.
The patient gradually developed dysphagia 21 mo ago. Esophagography and a chest contrast-enhanced computed tomography (CT) scan showed a tumor located in the middle and lower part of the esophagus . Esophageal gastroscopy examination was performed and biopsy pathology confirmed esophageal squamous cell carcinoma . Radical resection of esophageal cancer was performed 19.5 mo ago under general anesthesia. The patient complained again of dysphagia about 13.5 mo ago and benign anastomotic stenosis was diagnosed. A 20 mm in diameter and 60 mm in length esophageal covered SEMS was inserted after repeated balloon dilatation. The retrievable Z-stents are made of stainless steel and coated with silicon (Sigma Medical Industry Co., Ltd., Huaian, Jiangsu Province, China). Two weeks ago, the symptoms of dysphagia were significantly aggravated and esophagography showed severe stenosis above the proximal stent.
The patient had mild coronary heart disease for 15 years, but did not take medication.
The patient had no history of smoking, drinking, or familial cancers.
Vital signs were as follows: Body temperature 36 ℃, pulse 88 bpm, respiration 22 breaths/min, blood pressure 99/69 mmHg, height 169 cm, and body weight 50.0 kg. Oxygen saturation in room air was 99%.
Routine blood tests showed the following: Leukocyte count 4.73 × 109/L, erythrocyte count 3.80↓ × 1012/L, hemoglobin 117.0 g/L↓↓, Platelet count 202 × 109/L, neutrophil percentage 75.6%↑, and lymphocyte percentage 15.7%.
Severe stenosis above the proximal stent was diagnosed by esophagography . Removal of the stent was decided due to aggravated dysphagia after repeated balloon dilation.
