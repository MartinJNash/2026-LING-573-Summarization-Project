A 40-year-old male presented with a 3-year history of sensory changes involving the S2–S3 distribution in the right leg (e.g., hypoesthesia without motor weakness or dysuria). The lumbosacral MRI showed a noncontrast enhancing right-sided cystic mass at the S2–S3 level; it had the same intensity as CSF on both the T1- and T2-weighted images . Coronal fat-suppressed T2-weighted images revealed the mass likely originated from the right S3 nerve root and was additionally compressing the right S2 nerve root .
A laminoplastic laminotomy was performed at three levels using an ultrasonic bone curette. At surgery, the S3 nerve root was enveloped within cyst wall and the S2 nerve root was clearly compressed [Figure and ]. Partial resection of the cyst wall and imbrication of residual tissue was performed . An inlet from the subarachnoid space was identified, and its obliteration was confirmed by a Valsalva maneuver . This subarachnoid connection was sealed with adipose tissue and fibrin glue . Plication of the cyst wall was performed with nonpenetrating titanium clips (Vascular Clip System; LeMaitre Vascular Inc., Burlington, MA) . No postoperative CSF leakage occurred, and spinal lumbar drainage was not warranted. The patient's preoperative sensory disturbance resolved. The postoperative MRI showed a reduction in the cyst's size [Figure and ] and no residual compression of the S2 nerve root . Histopathological examination confirmed collagen connective tissue without nerve fibers, findings consistent with a Tarlov cyst . The patient remained asymptomatic 6 months later, and the 6-month postoperative sacral MRI demonstrated no cyst recurrence.
