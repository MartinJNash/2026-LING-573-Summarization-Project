On 12 August 2022, a 33-year-old black woman, gravida three, para two, a housewife, was taken to the obstetric care unit at 24 weeks of pregnancy with a 1-week history of lethargy, fever, and a dry cough. She had never had a stroke, trauma, alcoholism, obesity, drug misuse, a vehicle accident, respiratory symptoms, gallstones, or an underlying infection. She had no family history of diabetes mellitus or another chronic disease. She had only been diagnosed with type 1 diabetes 2 years earlier. For the previous 2 years, she had been using neutral protamine Hagedorn (NPH) insulin at 18 and 10 IU to manage her type 1 diabetes mellitus. She had not traveled in the 2 weeks prior to her admission date. She shared a house with her husband, who is a long-distance truck driver, and her young child, who attended kindergarten. Her husband had been sick for a week. He did go to the hospital for treatment for his condition, and tests revealed that he had a known COVID-19 exposure.
She arrived at the hospital with a 3-day history of weight loss and lethargy, as well as a 2-day history of shortness of breath, weak muscles, headache, agonizing stomach pain, epigastric pain, diarrhea, and excessive hunger. No vomiting signs were present in her. She had a mean arterial blood pressure of 123/79 mm Hg, a pulse rate of 114 beats per minute, a body temperature of 38.9°C, and a respiratory rate of 20 breaths per minute at the time of admission. She stood 1.63 mtall, weighed 73 kg, and had a BMI of 27.5 kg/m2, which is higher than the permitted range of 18.5–24.9 kg/m2.
Her emergency department blood tests at the time of her admission revealed fasting blood glucose of 182 mg/dl, random blood glucose of 209 mg/dl, a 2-h postprandial blood glucose of 217 mg/dl, a white blood cell count of 12 140 cells/mm3 (4500–11 000 cells/mm3), neutrophils 61%, serum amylase level was high at 483 U/l (40–140 U/l), serum lipase was at 397 U/l (0–160 U/l), serum creatinine of 1.6 mg/dl, serum sodium of 147 mEq/l, serum potassium of 5.4 mEq/l, and urine ketones of 2+ were detected during examination of the urine. Reverse transcription-PCR COVID-19 testing required a nasopharyngeal swab specimen, and the result revealed the presence of the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) virus. She received 5 l of intranasal oxygen per minute for 21 h before being transferred to an ICU. Finally, she was diagnosed with well-controlled type 1 diabetes mellitus, new-onset acute pancreatitis, and a confirmed COVID-19 infection. She was admitted to the ICU after her COVID-19 tests were positive.
At an ICU at transfer date, her vital signs were a body temperature of 38.4oC, a blood pressure of 111/78 mm Hg, a peripheral pulse rate of 112 beats per minute, a respiratory rate of 19 cycles per minute, and an oxygen saturation of 87% on room air.
Her blood investigations performed upon her admission in the emergency room revealed white blood cell count of 13 700 cells/mm3, neutrophils 65%, blood urea nitrogen of 28 mg/dl, fasting blood glucose of 200 mg/dl, a 2-h postprandial blood glucose of 227 mg/dl, serum creatinine of 1.4 mg/dl, serum sodium of 137 mEq/l, serum potassium of 5.0 mEq/l, pH arterial blood of 7.03, anion gap level of 13 mEq/l, partial pressure of carbon dioxide of 34 mm Hg, serum bicarbonate level of 4.6 mEq/l, serum phosphate level of 2.7 mg/dl, an aspartate aminotransferase level of 44 units/l, an alanine aminotransferase level of 51 units/l, an erythrocyte sedimentation rate of 17 mm/h, and urine analysis was positive for urine ketones of 2+.
An abdominal computed tomography scan revealed an atrophic pancreas with widespread fatty infiltration, little fluid and fat stranding around the pancreas, and reactive lymph nodes. Her arterial blood gas study at an ICU demonstrated compensated metabolic acidosis with a significant anion gap. An examination of the chest radiography revealed widespread bilateral infiltrates that were consistent with a COVID-19 infection as well as tiny pneumonic patches in the top and lower sections of the left lung. A slight expiratory wheeze in each bilateral lower lung area without rhonchi or rhales stood out during her lung examination. She had a soft, pregnant belly with no organomegaly that was notable for the palpable epigastric pain and fluctuating dullness. Her ECG revealed ST-depression in the anterior-lateral leads and sinus tachycardia with a heart rate of 112 beats per minute. The emergency room’s ultrasound revealed no evidence of cholelithiasis or gallbladder inflammation.
She started receiving fluid resuscitation (0.9% normal saline) and insulin delivery by drip as soon as she was admitted to an ICU. She had 24 h of insulin infusion therapy in addition to 60 mEq/L of potassium chloride administered intravenously. As part of the protocol for managing SARS-CoV-2, she continued oxygen inhalation with 5 l of oxygen as soon as she arrived. In order to prevent deep vein thromboembolism while she was infected with COVID-19, she was administered 80 mg of subcutaneous enoxaparin every 12 h. Over the course of 5 days, she underwent dexamethasone intravenously, 4 mg/2 ml, once daily. On day 13, when the patient’s blood sugar started to stabilize, syringe pump delivery of insulin was stopped, and subcutaneous insulin injections were once again offered. She received NPH insulin 18/10 after 13 days of insulin drip in order to improve her poor metabolic control. She was given 500 ml of normal saline with vitamin B complex and 40% glucose to treat her acute pancreatitis and stop the progression of acute respiratory distress syndrome. Omeprazole 40 mg injections were continuously administered to the patient for 3 days to treat acute pancreatitis. She received as much as 500 mg of acetaminophen as needed to treat her acute pancreatitis pain and fever from the SARS-CoV-2 virus.
After spending 17 days in the hospital, the patient was eventually released on 27 August 27 2022, after receiving back-to-back COVID-19 throat swab test results that were negative. She was released with her previous antidiabetic drugs, NPH insulin 18/10, and her blood glucose levels were close to normal. Her lipase levels were continuously checked while she was being released; they were close to normal, and she was given omeprazole 20 mg twice a day for 14 days. Her continued monthly follow-up at the ambulatory clinic was advised.
