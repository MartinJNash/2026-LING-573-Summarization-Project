A 24-year-old male presented with a 3-week history of intensifying morning headache, progressively worsening gate, unsteadiness, and blurred vision; he also reported increasing frequent episodes of transient loss of vision for a few seconds each time he stood up quickly, in the week before admission; on the day of admission, he presented acute-onset binocular diplopia.
Examination revealed horizontal binocular diplopia; the remainder of the cranial nerve examination findings were unremarkable; motor strength, sensation, and reflexes resulted normal, while minimal gate unsteadiness was noticed. A nontender and not painful lump, of which the patient was not aware, was palpable at the level of inion. The patient was referred to an ophthalmologist, who performed fundoscopy, which showed gross (Grade III Frisen scale) papilledema, and Hess screen chart, confirming right sixth cranial nerve palsy. A noncontrast-enhanced CT scan demonstrated a paramedian right occipital well-circumscribed, inhomogeneously hyperdense, lesion with sharply demarcated erosion of both tables of the skull and scalloping of sclerotic bony margins [ and ]; the 3D CT reconstruction clearly showed the bone defect . MRI confirmed the presence of the intradiploic, almost spherical, lesion with a diameter of about 2.6 cm; on T1-weighted images, the lesion appeared hypointense to the brain and slightly hyperintense to the CSF; on T2-weighted images, it appeared inhomogeneously hyperintense to the brain and almost isointense to CSF; diffusion-weighted images were hyperintense with a high value on apparent diffusion coefficient; contrast-enhanced T1-weighted images revealed almost complete occlusion of the venous flow at the confluence of sinuses (torcular herophili) by the lesion itself, which resulted nonenhanced [ and ]; MRI venogram confirmed the occlusion of the torcular . Neuroimaging was compatible with diagnosis of intradiploic epidermoid cyst with intralesional hemorrhage.
Considering the fast worsening of symptomatology, the presence of evident papilledema and the acute onset of diplopia from the right sixth cranial nerve palsy, all compatible with intracranial hypertension, a rapid restoration of flow in the main venous sinuses was reputed necessary and the patient was operated on immediately after completion of clinical and radiological assessment. A longitudinal median parieto-occipital incision of 8 cm, centered on the inion, allowed skeletonization of the region and exposure of the lesion, which bulged from the eroded outer bone table, harvested by a fibrotic capsule; incision of the capsule gave rise to a small amount of brownish fluid (collected for analysis) exposing the cyst content, which had a variegated appearance with some dark areas suggestive for previous subacute intralesional hemorrhage and other areas with the classical pearly aspect of epidermoid cysts; under microscopic vision, the lesion was removed piecemeal; the fibrotic capsule was separated from the sclerotic borders of the bone and dissected away from the intact dura mater, with evident blood flow restoration in the confluence of sinuses; thereafter, the sclerotic scalloped bone margins were drilled to reduce their thickness, for a better reconstruction, which was performed by acrylic resin cranioplasty [-].
Postoperative course was uneventful; the patient was mobilized on the 1st postoperative day and discharged on the 4th postoperative day; diplopia progressively improved, and at a 30 days follow-up, the sixth cranial nerve palsy and the papilledema had completely resolved. Postoperative CT scan with 3D reconstruction and MRI with MR venogram, performed during hospitalization, demonstrated the radical exeresis of the lesion, the optimal result of cranioplasty, and the restoration of flow in the torcular herophili and transverse sinuses [ and ].
Histology of specimen revealed features consistent with a cystic formation with a fibrous wall, outlined by squamous epithelium and containing keratinic material arranged in lamellae, consistent with the diagnosis of epidermoid cyst . Analysis of the cystic fluid revealed an exudate with erythrocytes, leukocytes, and epithelial cells.
