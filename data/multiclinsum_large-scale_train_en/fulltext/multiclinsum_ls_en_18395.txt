A 47-year-old Japanese woman with 23 years’ history of SLE had sudden onset of severe headache and was referred to our hospital. She had been treated as an out-patient with a daily prednisolone dose of 7.5mg and cyclophosphamide of 50mg/day before the onset. Her glomerular filtration rate (GFR) was 5.6mL/minute /1.73m2 and the stage was classified as G5 (end stage) of chronic kidney disease (CKD) on admission. Laboratory examinations revealed hypocomplementemia (complement C3: 66mg/dL, C4: 14mg/dL). Her anti-double-stranded deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) antibody (IgG) was 6.5IU/mL (normal range <20). Computed tomography (CT) demonstrated SAH in her right Sylvian fissure . Considering that she had SLE and had developed chronic renal failure without renal dialysis, MRA was used to identify her ruptured aneurysm. MRA was performed using a 1.5T magnetic resonance system (Signa HDxt, GE, USA). Contrast material was not used. The volume rendering (VR) method with three-dimensional time-of-flight (TOF) MRA was performed in addition to the usual maximum intensity projection (MIP) image . MRA revealed a fenestration at the M1 portion of her right MCA and a small saccular aneurysm at the proximal end of the fenestration . This was consistent with a ruptured aneurysm. A left frontotemporal craniotomy was performed to clip the neck of her aneurysm on the same day. The fenestration and ruptured aneurysm were confirmed under surgical microscopy, and the aneurysm was successfully occluded . Intraoperative indocyanine green videoangiography confirmed this result . The present patient was carefully managed avoiding cerebral vasospasm and general complications during the perioperative period and renal dialysis was initiated after the acute phase and had a favorable outcome. Postoperatively, she developed normal pressure hydrocephalus, requiring placement of a ventriculoperitoneal shunt. She was ultimately discharged home in good condition.
