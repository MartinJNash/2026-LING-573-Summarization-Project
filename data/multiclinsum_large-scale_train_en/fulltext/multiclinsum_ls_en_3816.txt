Mr. M is a single 18-year-old male with no psychiatric history. Although he started smoking both regular cigarettes and e-cigarettes in junior high school, he had never vaped delta-9 THC or THC-O until the present episode. In August 2022, he purchased THC-O online and inhaled it for the first time using an e-cigarette device. After 20 min of inhalation, the patient experienced a sudden attack that lasted 2 h and was characterized by palpitations, shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, feelings of unreality, and fear of dying. He went to the hospital emergency room due to his urgent condition. He was worried and complained of difficulty breathing. His body temperature was 37.8°C, and his heart rate had climbed to 101 bpm. Sinus tachycardia was discovered on electrocardiogram (ECG) at admission. His other vitals were stable. Complete blood counts, tests of electrolyte levels and liver and renal function, urinalysis, and chest X-rays from the diagnostic workup were all within normal ranges (WNL). There was no history of head trauma or other medical conditions. He was brought to us, and a diagnosis of panic attack was made. Due to his persistent concern about additional attacks, the patient was given sertraline (25 mg). However, a few days after the prescription, he revisited our office and hoped to switch from sertraline to herbal medicine due to his concerns regarding the side effects of the antidepressant. Based on a shared decision-making process, herbal medicine (Hangekobokuto) was prescribed to the patient. After the presented episode, he did not inhale THC-O. Throughout the subsequent 6 months of follow-up, he maintained improvement with no panic attacks, and his concern about additional attacks had been alleviated.
