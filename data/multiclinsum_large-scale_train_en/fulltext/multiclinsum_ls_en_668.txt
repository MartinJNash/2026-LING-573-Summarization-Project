In 2008, a man aged 64 years was admitted to the emergency department with symptoms of pain and swelling in both knees and inability to walk. The history of the present condition was a fall from one step with minimal trauma.
Physical examination revealed tenderness and a gap in both suprapatellar regions, no active motion in the extensor mechanism, and palpable defects in the quadriceps tendons with respect to attachment to the patella. There was only back pain in his medical history, which was treated with NSAIDs for ten years. There was no history of steroid use, fluoroquinolone antibiotic use, steroid injection, or chronic life-threatening disease. Initial X-rays were obtained. There was an osteophyte in the tibial tuberosity in the lateral left knee X-rays. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was performed for further imaging. MRI showed the bilateral total rupture of the quadriceps tendons and showed the osteophyte in the tibial tuberosity in the left knee . A complete analysis revealed ossification and calcification of the anterior vertebral column in the spinal X-rays .
Laboratory tests were normal and showed a total leukocyte count (WBC) of 8600/mm3, an erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of 13 mm/h, and C-reactive protein (CRP) of 1.8 mg/l. Renal function was normal with creatinine (Cr) of 1.3 mg/dl and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) of 19 mg/dl. The patient's blood pressure was normal and blood sugar level was also normal (glucose: 83 mg/dl). HBA1C was not known. Cholesterol levels were normal (HDL: 57 mg/dl; LDL: 126 mg/dl). The patient has no metabolic syndrome. BMI was detected to be 24. The patient consulted the rheumatology department, and diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis was diagnosed when clinical and radiologic data were analyzed.
