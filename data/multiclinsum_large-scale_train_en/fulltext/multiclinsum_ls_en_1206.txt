A 67-year-old female presented to the outpatient department with alleged history of fall and acute pain in the right hip. She was diagnosed with a right-sided transcervical neck of femur fracture and was admitted to the hospital . She also had a history of schizophrenia and was on anti-psychotic drug therapy for the same.
An uncemented Dual-Mobility Total Hip Replacement (DM-THR) was planned after discussion with the patient and her relatives. The surgery was performed in the lateral decubitus position using the posterolateral approach to the hip. The following component sizes were used: 46 mm acetabular shell, 26 mm (+1.5 mm) stainless steel femur head, UHMWPE 46 (26) liner, and femoral stem of size 12. There were no intraoperative complications and the patient was mobilized full-weight-bearing with a walker. The post-operative radiograph is shown in .
At 3 weeks after surgery, the patient slipped and fell off her bed. Radiological examination revealed posterior dislocation of the DM-THA . Closed reduction was performed under general anesthesia and a boot and bar cast was given to the patient.
However, she started complaining of hip pain 2 days later. A fresh radiograph was taken which again showed a posteriorly dislocated THA.
IPD of the dual mobility THR was suspected and a CT was ordered.
Meanwhile, on revisiting the previous X-ray, eccentric position of the femoral head within the acetabular shell raised the suspicion of an IPD. The polyethylene (PE) liner silhouette was also seen superolaterally over the hip joint. The CT scan confirmed the diagnosis .
We suspected infection as the cause of the initial dislocation of the THA and managed our case with open reduction, debridement, change of the PE liner, and insertion of antibiotic cement beads. Intraoperatively, the PE liner was seen dislocated and lying posterosuperiorly after removing the head . The revision was successful with no further dislocations within the 1-year follow-up period .
