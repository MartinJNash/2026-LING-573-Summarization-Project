An 80-year-old osteoporotic female with a prior history of a T12 balloon kyphoplasty had fallen 10 days earlier. The thoracolumbar CT showed the left TP fractures from L1 to 3, a T10 chance fracture, and multiple osteoporotic fractures involving the T11, T12, L2, L3, and L4 vertebrae [ and ]. The magnetic resonance image also showed that the T10 vertebra was hyperintense on the short-tau inversion recovery study, and low-intensity on T1-weighting images, confirming the diagnosis of an acute T10 fracture [ and ].
With an unstable vertebral fracture of T10, the patient underwent a minimally invasive thoracolumbar T8–L3 PPS fusion under C-arm fluoroscopy [ and ]. Postoperatively, when the patient was turned back to the supine position, she suddenly went into shock. Her acute anemia (hemoglobin [Hb] 6.0g/dL and preoperative Hb 12.0g/dL) warranted urgent transfusions of 6 units of red blood cells. The CT angiogram documented the leakage of contrast ventral to the left L3 TP where there was also a large retroperitoneal hematoma ; notably, the left L3 PS was accurately placed within the pedicle . When urgent angiography documented a pseudoaneurysm involving the left third lumbar artery, TAE was performed (i.e., using 0.3 mL of 33% n-butyl-2-cyanoacrylate injected from the proximal portion of the lumbar artery) . The retroperitoneal hematoma was spontaneously absorbed over time, and the patient was transferred to a rehabilitation hospital 3 weeks later, following up with outpatient visits within 3 postoperative months.
