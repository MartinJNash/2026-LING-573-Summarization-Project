A 70-year-old male patient with a history of extensive nicotine abuse, laryngeal squamous cell carcinoma seven years ago, bladder urothelial carcinoma ten years ago, and a dual-chamber pacemaker (type Medtronic Azure XT W2DR01 MRI Surescan, an RV lead type Medtronic 5076 Capsure Fix Novus MRI 59 cm and an RA lead type Medtronic 5076 Capsure Fix Novus MRI 52 cm) implanted 9 months ago for sick sinus syndrome, was seen routinely in the outpatient cardiology clinic. He had complaints of persisting fatigue, dyspnoea, and coughing. He had been tested positive for COVID-19 infection three weeks earlier, but strangely his symptoms did not abate with time and were rather progressive. He also noted an evident, new swelling of his pacemaker pocket ( and ). At the outpatient clinic, the patient had normal blood pressure, normal heart rate, no signs of cardiac decompensation or cachexia. Chest ultrasound suggested the presence of a soft tissue mass with increased vascularity, rather than a collection or a late haematoma ( and ). The mass was also identified using conventional CT imaging . Ultrasound-guided biopsy of the mass was obtained for histopathology and revealed a well differentiated invasive squamous cell carcinoma . Additional PD-L1 staining was moderately positive . PET-CT scan demonstrated FDG-avid lesions in the left lung hilum (, ) with multiple FDG-avid lesions present in the liver, as well as mediastinal lymph nodes and bone lesions. Furthermore, several soft tissue masses, among which a large mass surrounding the pacemaker pocket, were identified .
The patient was subsequently referred for an oncologic assessment, but he refused to undergo further treatment and chose for palliative care. He died 1.5 months after his cardiology outpatient consultation where the mass was first noticed.
