A 54-year-old male presented to our hospital with complaints of pain and progressive multiple swellings in the right hand and wrist for the past 6 months. No history of trauma. No loss of appetite and weight loss. There was no contact history of tuberculosis.
On local examination, one swelling of size 5×4×2 cm presents on dorsum of hand . Two swellings of size 2×1×1cm present over dorsolateral aspect of wrist with positive cross fluctuation test. Movements of Rt. wrist active painless palmar flexion of 0–60 and 60–70 painful with 10° of restriction, active painless dorsi flexion 0–60 and 60–70 painful with 20° of restriction, radial deviation of 0–10, and ulnar deviation of 0–20. There was no palpable lyphadenopathy. No distal neurovascular deficits.
All hematological and biochemical investigations were in normal range except ESR and CRP were elevated. Radiological investigations on X-ray show no bony abnormalities detected and ultrasound report suggestive of extensor tendon compartment sheath effusion with synovial hypertrophy likely tenosynovitis. The patient was posted for biopsy and excision of the affected tissue. A histopathological examination reveals an epitheloid granuloma with Langhans type large cells and central necrosis that may be Koch’s etiology for Chronic Granulomatous Synovitis (, ).
Following which patient was subjected to Mantoux test which tested positive and patient was started on antitubercular therapy after histopathological report. Active movements of hand and wrist started immediate postoperatively as tolerated by patient. Antitubercular therapy was continued for 6 months. Post-operative follow-up was uneventful and improved functionally, no recurrence, and no pain with normal range of motion.
