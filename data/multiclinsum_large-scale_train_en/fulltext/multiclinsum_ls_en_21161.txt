A 23-year-old woman presented to the emergency department due to worsening right upper quadrant pain for a few weeks, with new onset nausea and emesis with eating, and blood-streaked diarrhea. Diarrhea was of recent onset; however, rectal bleeding is known for this patient and was diagnosed as constipation in a past visit by her primary care doctor.
Her previous medical and surgical history was unremarkable. Family history was unknown except for an identical twin sister who had bowel resection at the age of 12 for similar bowel obstructive symptoms. The sister could not recall the exact indication for her past surgery, but recognized intussusception when asked. The patient is a daily cigarette smoker and occasional social drinker.
On admission, the patient was afebrile, with normal and stable vital signs. On the physical exam, her abdomen was soft, obese, and nondistended with epigastric and right upper quadrant tenderness.
Routine blood tests (complete blood count (CBC) and complete metabolic panel) were within normal limits except for carbon dioxide of 23.0 mmol/L. Urinalysis with reflex microscopic and reflex culture was significant for trace ketones, 1+ leukocyte esterase, white blood cells 5-10/HPF (normal 0-4/HPF), 1+ mucous, and occasional bacteria.
Follow-up CBC performed on hospital day 1 was significant for a red blood cell count of 3.5 x106/ul (from 4.68 x106/ul), hemoglobin of 10.5 g/dl (from 14.2 g/dl), and hematocrit of 31.5% (from 41.4%).
Computed tomography (CT) of the abdomen and pelvis with oral contrast demonstrated focal intussusception of the distal small bowel on a background of a lobulated intraluminal small bowel filling defect concerning for a solid mass as the lead point .
Due to the obstructive symptoms and indications of gastrointestinal bleeding, a surgical consultation was obtained.
