A 66-year-old male patient with diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia presented to the emergency department of our hospital with a sudden onset of walking difficulty (ataxia). On examination, the patient was fully conscious and oriented to time, person, and place (GCS of 15). His speech was fluent, and his comprehension and repetition were intact. Pupils were isocoric and reactive to light. There was no facial asymmetry or eye movement restrictions. The examination showed full muscle strength in all four extremities (5/5 based on the Medical Research Council score). All sensory modalities were intact. Deep tendon reflexes were normal. Further neurological examination did not reveal dysmetria or dysdiadochokinesia. The Romberg test and tandem gait test were positive with the tendency to sway to the left side. A noncontrast brain computed tomography was negative for intracerebral hemorrhage. Diffusion-weighed MRI did not show any acute cerebral infarct. The cervical MRI did not find any pathological findings explaining the condition. A nerve conduction study and EMG excluded peripheral neuropathy that may have caused acute ataxia. The patient was admitted for further evaluation of the potential etiology of ataxia. A brain MRI with contrast revealed a homogeneously contrast enhancing mass lesion of 14×16×12 mm (2.5 cm3) in the left CPA with minimal extension to the left internal acoustic canal (see Figs. & ). The neurosurgery department did not recommend urgent surgical intervention; instead, follow up was recommended. After 1 week of intrahospital treatment and balance learning exercises, he was discharged to home. This case has been reported in line with the Surgical CAse REport (SCARE) 2020 criteria.
