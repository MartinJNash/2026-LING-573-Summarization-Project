A 36-year-old healthy female was admitted to our hospital due to the sudden onset of a severe headache. Gadolinium-enhanced magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) revealed an enhanced mass in the cerebellar vermis . Four days later, her consciousness became impaired and she suffered generalized seizures. Brain computed tomography (CT) showed subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) in the posterior fossa . We performed cerebral angiography to ascertain the peritumoral angioarchitecture. A left vertebral angiogram showed a tumor stain in the cerebellar vermis fed by the posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA) and an aneurysm on the distal portion of the feeder . We considered a flow-related aneurysm as the origin of the hemorrhage and performed endovascular treatment of both the feeder and the aneurysm using a N-butyl cyanoacrylate lipiodol mixture to avoid rebleeding and to minimize intraoperative blood loss. Under general anesthesia, a 5 Fr Guider™ guiding catheter (Stryker, Fremont, California, USA) was inserted in the left vertebral artery. After positioning a Magic™ micro catheter (BALT, Montmorency, France) in the distal portion of the vermian branch of PICA just proximal to the aneurysm, we carefully injected a 20% NBCA-lipiodol mixture into the aneurysm and the distal portion of the main feeder. Postoperative left vertebral angiograms confirmed the complete disappearance of the aneurysm; the anterior part of the tumor stain was clearly diminished [Figure –]. Her clinical condition gradually improved and she suffered no recurrent hemorrhage. Four days later, we removed the vascular-rich tumor attached to the cerebellar vermis and the embolized distal PICA aneurysm . Pathological findings confirmed our preoperative diagnosis of cerebellar hemangioblastoma and intracerebral aneurysm, and showed disruption of the internal elastic lamina and rupture of the adventitia .
