A 16-year-old boy while playing football suddenly developed acute onset of pain in the left knee and inability to bear weight on the left leg while he ran and kicked the ball in his right foot. Patient was brought to emergency immediately. On presentation, patient had massive swelling of the left knee mainly at infrapatellar region along with tenderness and loss of active extension. Proximal half of the leg was swollen and tense in anterior aspect with no open injuries and neurovascular deficit. X-ray shown an Ogden Type IV fracture of tibial tuberosity . Due to imminent risk of development of compartment, patient was taken for surgical correction immediately.
Anterior midline approach was utilized. Extensive soft-tissue disruption was identified; however, patellar tendon was intact. Soft-tissue interposition was found at fracture site. Interposed soft tissue was released using traction and manipulation at fracture site. Fracture was reduced and fixed using 02 × 4 mm partially threaded cannulated cancellous screws .
Wound closure done in layers and a suction drain was placed followed by application of above knee POP slab. However, prophylactic fasciotomy was not carried out.
Patient developed few hemorrhagic blisters at fracture site during post-operative period which was managed with oral antibiotics and local wound care. Above knee slab was converted to a cast after wound healing. Patient was kept on non-weight bearing ambulation up to 6 weeks until the removal of above knee cast. Later, knee flexion was initiated along with progressive weight bearing ambulation as tolerable by the patient. He was full weight bearing ambulant by end of 12 weeks. Follow-up was done at 6 weeks, 12 weeks, and at 6 months ( and ). Patient had regained complete ROM of knee and returned to sporting activities by end of 6 months ( and ).
