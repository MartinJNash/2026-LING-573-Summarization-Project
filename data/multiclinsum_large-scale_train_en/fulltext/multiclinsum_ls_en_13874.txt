An 80-year-old male underwent coiling of bilateral carotid artery aneurysms [-]. One year later, he developed progressive gait disturbance and cognitive dysfunction consistent with NPH. This was confirmed on brain computed tomography (CT), that showed ventricular enlargement with periventricular lucency. To reduce the risk of dual antiplatelet therapy (i.e., aspirin and clopidogrel) and intracranial hemorrhage associated with placing a VP shunt, the patient received an LP shunt (i.e., at the CT-documented “wider” L2/L3 level) [-]. The shunt included a programmable valve with plastic connectors and an antisiphon device (pressure setting 4, 110 mmH2O; outer diameter of catheter, 1.65 mm).
Postoperatively, his gait/cognitive function improved, and the ventricles decreased in size. However, six months later, symptoms recurred, and the CT once again showed enlarged ventricles/ periventricular lucency, plus L2 intrathecal migration of the lumbar catheter [- and -]. A VP shunt was placed, and within five days, the patient was clinically improved (i.e., Mini-Mental State Examination score improved from 19/30 to 28/30).
