A 53-year-old woman presented to the Cardiology Department of our hospital complaining of episodic chest discomfort.
She had experienced episodic chest discomfort for the past month with symptoms arising when she was agitated or when she overexerted herself.
The patient had a medical history of asthma.
There was no obvious abnormality in the patient's physical examination.
Routine blood tests, routine urine tests, routine fecal tests, blood biochemistry, immune indexes, and infection indexes were normal. Chest radiography and echocardiography were unremarkable. However, the electrocardiogram showed occasional premature ventricular beats, and the treadmill test was positive.
Coronary angiography showed that no stenosis was observed in the left or right coronary artery . However, “ghostlike” intermittent appearance of coronary ventricular fistula and collateral branching was observed. The right coronary ventricular fistula and collateral circulation appeared abruptly , and then was undetectable . Nevertheless, the patient refused the computed tomography angiography because of her medical insurance.
