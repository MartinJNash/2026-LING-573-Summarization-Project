A 30-year-old female was referred to the emergency room (ER) with a major complaint of sudden thunderclap headache a day before admission. This was the worst headache, with a numeric rating scale (NRS) of 10. The patient also complained of double vision and left ptosis that lasted for 1 day. There was no other complaint or history of hypertension, diabetes, or trauma.
On arrival at the ER, the headache had gotten better with NRS 1–2. Non-contrast head computed tomography (CT) showed ICH, SAH, and SDH on the left side of the brain, which was not consistent with a hypertensive pattern. The secondary ICH had a score of 6, indicating that 100% of the bleeding is caused by underlying vascular malformation. Cerebral angiography showed a plexiform-type cortical AVM with an intranidal aneurysm on the left occipital lobe. The AVM is supplied by a feeder artery from the P4 distal branch of the left posterior cerebral artery and drained by a vein that connects to the occipital cortical vein and superior sagittal sinus. Curative embolization was done 2 days later. First, a microcatheter over microwire was inserted into the feeder artery, and adhesive liquid n-BCA glue (Lipiodol 1:2) with 30% concentration was injected twice, followed by 1:3 with 25% concentration once. The feeder artery was occluded and getting collateral from the pial vessels of the left middle cerebral artery. The procedure was carried out for curative purposes, and complete obliteration of the AVM was achieved . After 14 days, the second head CT showed a significant reduction and absorption of the bleeding, and the patient was discharged in better condition . The value of headache NRS ranged from 0 to 1, and there were neither new neurological deficits nor complaints after the procedure.
