A 70-year-old man with an 8-month history of left posterior thigh and leg pain was admitted to our Emergency Department after a fall during a gym session. He presented with a moderate pelvic and head trauma. A physical examination showed only tenderness upon palpation and percussion of the lumbar and sacral spine. Plain radiographic examinations of spine, pelvis and chest were interpreted as normal. The patient had no medical or surgical history other than essential hypertension. A few hours after admission, he became very confused and agitated. A cerebral computed tomography scan did not show either vascular lesion or cerebral contusion but fat droplets in the lateral ventricles . A further investigation with CT scan of the spine revealed a fractured sacrum extending into a ruptured perineurial cyst . A cerebral and spinal magnetic resonance image (MRI) scan confirmed these findings and we suspected that fatty bone marrow had migrated from sacral fracture to the brain in an unusual way: a dural breach at the Tarlov cyst. Surgical treatment was not carried out because of the fractured sacrum. The patient remained under medical observation and fully recovered within three weeks. Two months after discharge, the patient had no complaints and had a normal physical neurological examination.
