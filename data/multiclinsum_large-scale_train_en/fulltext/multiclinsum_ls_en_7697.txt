A 17-year-old male patient sustained a minor ankle trauma while skating. He was first admitted to a local health department. Initial findings were a 1cm long wound in relation with his left lateral malleolus together with moderate swelling. No other sign or symptom was reported. Plain radiographs were performed with no pathological findings. About 6 hours later, the patient was admitted at our emergency room with disproportional limb pain, increased swelling, persistent fever, paleness, tachycardia, and hypotension ( and ). Blood tests showed anemia, leukocytosis, and elevated creatine phosphokinase . The general condition of the patient got worse, with the need of mechanical ventilation and vasopressors due to the hemodynamic instability. Empiric antibiotic therapy was established with a triple scheme (ceftriaxone, vancomycin, and clindamycin). A computed tomography (CT) scan was done to find the presence of collections (, , ). The patient underwent his first surgical procedure which consisted of double fasciotomy of the leg, toexamine and release the tension of the four compartments involved ( and ). Mubarak and Owen technique was used. Purulent material was obtained with no signs of necrosis. Drainage of the medial thigh compartment was performed at the same procedure. A vacuum-assisted closure device was used. Both leg incisions were primary closed ( and ). Multiple samples were forwarded to cultivation. Culture media demonstrated the presence of Streptococcus Pyogenes. The acute toxic-shock syndrome yielded immediately after fasciotomy. Twenty-Four hours later, the patient did no longer need mechanical ventilation and blood tests showed almost normal results. The patient underwent two more surgical procedures with the purpose of vacuum-assisted closure management and thigh wound healing. 1 week later, the patient was discharged from our institution. After 3 years of follow-up, the patient has fully recovered, with a functional limb with no restrictions.
