A 38-year-old right-hand dominant female patient with MS was admitted to our institution addressing bilateral elbow and wrist pain for the past 2 years. The patient had no history of upper limb trauma. Her symptoms increased during manual activities. Clinically, exquisite and snapping pain could be reproduced when the patient pronates her forearms applying wrist flexion and ulnar deviation. When achieving full pronosupination, palpable and visible ECU dislocation was equally addressed in both limbs . When reaching 30 degrees of elbow flexion, UN subluxed anteriorly sliding over the medial epicondyle; this phenomenon was found to be bilateral . Paresthesia along UN territory was constant. There was no motor deficit along with UN distribution.
The patient’s initial assessment included visual analog scale (VAS), Quick-Disability of the Arm, Shoulder, and Hand (Q-DASH), and the modified American Shoulder and Elbow Surgeons (m-ASES)scores. VAS for wrists and elbows happened to be 7 and 5, respectively. Pre-operative Q-DASH and m-ASES scores were 33 and 45, respectively.
Plain radiographs were performed with no pathological findings. A magnetic resonance imaging disclosed bilateral ECU dislocation together with tendonitis and tendinosis .
Due to the insufficient mechanical containment of both ECU and UN, bilateral surgical treatment was offered. Limbs were planned to be treated separately; first, the dominant side. Surgical procedure consists of two acts: First, ECU tenoplasty and 3months later UN transposition. Then, the same surgical procedure was performed on the contralateral limb.
