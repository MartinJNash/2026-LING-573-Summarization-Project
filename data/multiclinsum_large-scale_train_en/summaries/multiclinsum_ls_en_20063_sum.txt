A 49-year-old female with a history of HOCM and recently diagnosed scleroderma presented to the clinic with progressive dyspnoea. Transthoracic echocardiogram demonstrated left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction at rest, and elevated pulmonary artery (PA) pressure. Cardiac catheterization (CC) demonstrated an LVOT gradient of 150 mmHg, PA pressure of 88/32 mmHg, pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) 12 mmHg, pulmonary vascular resistance 14.8 Wu, and a cardiac index of 1.6 L/min/m2. The differential diagnosis for the dyspnoea included combined pre- and post-capillary pulmonary hypertension from longstanding HOCM vs. scleroderma associated PAH. Tadalafil was added to the patient's medical regimen of metoprolol but it was stopped because the patient developed pulmonary oedema. Alcohol septal ablation was undertaken with improvement in the LVOT gradient, but only a modest improvement in her dyspnoea. Repeat CC demonstrated worsening PAH. Vasodilatory testing with nitric oxide led to an improvement in the PA pressure with minimal increase of the PCWP. Hence, she was started on treprostinil and macitentan, with significant improvement in her dyspnoea on follow-up.
