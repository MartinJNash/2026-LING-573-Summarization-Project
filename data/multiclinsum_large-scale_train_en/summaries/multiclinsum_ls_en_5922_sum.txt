A 6-year-old boy with a family history of Huntington's disease, who was the only child of an unscreened and asymptomatic mother, was brought for a consultation because of migratory arthralgia, depressed mood, and rapid, abrupt and unintentional movements of his right arm and leg, that had evolved over a three-week period. On physical examination, he presented a grade III/VI systolic heart murmur and right-side choreic movements, giving rise to a deficit of active mobilization. Laboratory tests revealed elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (63 mm/h), C-reactive protein (25 mg/l) and antistreptolysin O titer (1,824 U/ml). Cardiovascular evaluation showed mild aortic insufficiency, moderate mitral insufficiency and a prolonged PR interval. A clinical diagnosis of Sydenham's chorea/acute rheumatic fever was made, and therapy consisting of penicillin, haloperidol, captopril and furosemide was instituted, with excellent results.
