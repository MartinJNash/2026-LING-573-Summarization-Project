A 68-year-old female presented to the emergency department with signs of urosepsis. Computed tomography (CT) imaging revealed a left-sided staghorn calculus with concurrent smaller renal calculi. Due to worsening kidney function during hospitalization, repeat imaging was done, which revealed a staghorn calculus in the rectum. The staghorn calculus migrated to the colon through a renocolic fistula, and the patient subsequently passed the large staghorn through the rectum spontaneously. Conservative management was pursued due to her high surgical risk. Several months after discharge, she represented with signs of infection, and a large left-sided psoas abscess was identified.
