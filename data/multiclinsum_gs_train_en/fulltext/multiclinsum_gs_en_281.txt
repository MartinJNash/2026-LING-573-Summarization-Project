A 35-year-old woman with recent use of a JAK inhibitor for rheumatoid arthritis presented with a two-week history of fever, cervical adenopathy, and facial erythema. On admission, she was found to have anemia, hypoalbuminemia, proteinuria, and severe acute renal injury. SLE was diagnosed, and parvovirus B19 DNA was detected in serum samples. A renal biopsy revealed GC without any typical features of lupus nephritis. The patient was treated with prednisone and showed marked improvement in anemia and renal function after a few weeks. In this case, the patient with SLE had GC possibly caused by parvovirus B19 infection associated with the homozygous G1 genotype of apolipoprotein 1 (APOL1), which has been described as a major risk factor for this glomerulopathy. Whether SLE had a causal relationship to the glomerular disease or was a concomitant cause is unclear. Treatment may be challenging in this context, since no antiviral medications are effective and immunosuppression has no discernible benefits, although the use of steroids was effective in treating the renal manifestations in this case.
